After 10 years as an academic researcher at UCSD and Keio University, I made the leap to freelance data science. Here are my honest reflections on what that transition looks like from the inside.

## What changes immediately

The most obvious change is the absence of a fixed structure. In academia, your calendar is anchored by semesters, lab meetings, conference deadlines, and grant cycles. As a freelancer, you build your own structure — or it builds itself around you in ways you might not expect.

Client communication is also a significant shift. Academic writing is dense and precise; client communication needs to be clear, concise, and outcome-focused. The same analysis that would earn a paragraph in a paper needs to fit in a two-sentence Slack message.

## What stays the same

The core skill — thinking rigorously about data — doesn't change at all. If anything, it becomes more valuable. Clients are paying for the ability to ask the right questions, design the right analysis, and interpret results without over-claiming.

The habit of reading literature also stays useful. Staying current on methods means I can offer approaches that aren't yet mainstream in industry.

## What I wish I'd known

Start building your online presence earlier than you think you need to. The gap between "thinking about freelancing" and "having enough work to be busy" is largely filled by visibility — a website, a GitHub profile, a few published blog posts.

Also: the jump is less scary than it looks from the inside.
